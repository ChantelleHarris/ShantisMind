Four Years Ago, This Dream

Four Years Ago, This Dream

by Chantelle Willow Spirit

×
Photo by   Glen Carrie   on   Unsplash

 I was living in some type of “project,“ I think. But the apartment I was in belonged to an Arab family. I was just kinda there; maybe I was the help. It was on the top floor of a crooked building—no security, but no one would rob a place so poor. The project had a tribal, pre-“our era” feel to it. Imagine a big project building surrounded by an indigenous village and its people. Misplaced is the word. The entrance to the building was a long, crude tunnel made of sticks with dirty, tannish brown fabric covering. The fabric was torn, of course, and the sun shone through. This tunnel, though, was deserted, as if no one ever came there, as if no one ever left.

 Suddenly, I was in a place that I frequented, structured like a school or church. I was a student, but not like continuing education. I was a student in a children’s school setting, and I was an adult—as I look today.

 Something made me angry, and I don’t remember what. The man in catholic priest garb was taking collection, and I was furious at him. I was protesting and screaming something at him, but he just laughed and walked away. Everyone else oblivious.

So, I stole the bulk of the collection money and left like I would normally, on foot.

 I was back home, and the Arab family was moving to Calhoun street. The landlord is allowing me to take over the lease. I stood there after they left, and the house was completely empty. No furniture—nothing. The sun was bright. Everything was tan. I’m leaving. I'm going to leave. As I descended, I passed back through the school church place. Everyone was frantic about the lost money. Catholic Man was furious and searching everyone. He suspected me. At once, a mass of tribal children from the village came to hide me from Catholic Man. Instantly, I was in my van in the midst of the frantic people, but it was so noisy, and I was struggling to keep the lights off or the music low or something. Whatever it was, the van drew attention to me. He saw me. He wanted to search me.

 I had the money hidden in my bra, and I watched myself put up such an argument to evade him. Then, I was on foot again, and I ran. Having reached the tunnel connected to the entrance of the building, I stopped. Everything stopped. There was no paved ground, but brown, cool Earth under my feet. It was quiet there and windy. It was calm and peaceful. It felt like home and freedom. I belong here. No one from the building would find me here. Ever.

 Then the children were there. They were dirty and barefoot, dressed in the same tattered fabric of the tunnel. Their fabric was dark green, however, like the forest. Perhaps I was, too, dressed exactly as they were. They led me to the spaceship that frequented the area. It always sealed itself shut when transporting the children. I was big on the ship, but I was on it. It sealed itself around me as well as I fell asleep. The drift was one of those intoxicating, warm, care-free drifts as I passed into oblivion. I left everything behind, and I knew that I was safe and that everything was going to be alright. This was surrender. I was happily at the beginning of my journey to freedom.

Asatoma Sadgamaya

Om Asatoma Sadgamaya

by Chantelle Willow Spirit

×

How I love India's culture and it's people, rich in beauty and spirituality. I promise to visit, for there is much I want to learn. There is much I want to unlearn. So, today, on my mind is this:

Om Asatoma Sadgamaya

Translation:

"Keep me not in the phenomenal world of Unreality. Make me go towards the Reality of the Eternal Self. Keep me not in the world of mortality. Make me go towards the world of immortality...of self-realization."
